Xiongqi (熊棋), also known as "Panda Chess", is an abstract strategy game derived from Xiangqi (Chinese chess) and adapted for an 8×8 board.
This document describes the necessary and sufficient rules to implement the game without ambiguity.

1. General Concepts

  • The game is played between two players: the South player and the North player.
  • The South player always makes the first move.
  • Players then alternate, making one move each.
  • A move consists of moving one piece of one's own side according to the movement rules described below.
  • There is no "pass": on their turn, a player must either make a legal move, or the game ends.
  • The key piece in Xiongqi is the general: its capture or the inability to defend it ends the game.

2. Board and Initial Setup

2.1 Board

  • Xiongqi is played on a board of 8 columns by 8 ranks (8×8).
  • From the South player's perspective, columns are labelled left to right with letters: a, b, c, d, e, f, g, h.
  • From the South player's perspective, ranks are numbered bottom to top: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8.
  • The South player occupies the ranks near 1, the North player occupies those near 8.
  • The "river" lies between ranks 4 and 5.

2.2 Pieces

Each side has the following pieces:

  • 1 general
  • 1 guard
  • 2 chariots
  • 2 bears
  • 2 horses
  • 2 cannons
  • 8 soldiers

2.3 Initial Setup

The initial setup is structurally identical for both sides, arranged as a mirror image.

2.3.1 South Side

  • Rank 1 (from a1 to h1): chariot, horse, bear, guard, general, bear, horse, chariot.
  • Rank 2: a cannon on c2 and a cannon on f2; other squares are empty.
  • Rank 3: a soldier on each square (a3 to h3).

2.3.2 North Side

  • Rank 8 (from a8 to h8): chariot, horse, bear, guard, general, bear, horse, chariot.
  • Rank 7: a cannon on c7 and a cannon on f7; other squares are empty.
  • Rank 6: a soldier on each square (a6 to h6).

2.4 Piece Notation Conventions

By convention, each piece type is associated with a Chinese character and a distinct Latin letter abbreviation for the South side (uppercase) and the North side (lowercase). These conventions are used in certain notation systems and implementations, but do not affect movement rules in any way.

3. Movement and Capture Principles

  • On their turn, a player moves one piece of their own side.
  • A move goes from a starting square to a destination square.
  • A piece cannot move to a square occupied by a piece of its own side.
  • If the destination square contains an enemy piece, that piece is captured and removed from the board.
  • Unless otherwise stated, pieces cannot "jump" over other pieces: all intermediate squares must be empty.
  • A move is illegal if it places or leaves the active player's general in check at the end of the move.

3.1 General

  • The general moves one square horizontally or vertically.
  • It captures by moving to a square occupied by an adjacent enemy piece.
  • It is not confined to a palace: it can move freely to any legal square on the board.
  • A general can never end its move on a square attacked by the opponent.

3.2 Guard

  • The guard moves one square diagonally.
  • It captures by moving to a diagonally adjacent square occupied by an enemy piece.
  • It is not confined to a palace: it can move freely to any legal square on the board.

3.3 Chariot

  • The chariot moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically.
  • It cannot jump over other pieces.
  • It captures by moving to a square occupied by an enemy piece on the same rank or column.

3.4 Bear

  • The bear moves any number of squares diagonally.
  • It cannot jump over other pieces.
  • It captures by moving to a square occupied by an enemy piece on the same diagonal.

3.5 Horse

  • The horse's movement is performed in two steps:
    1. one square in an orthogonal direction (up, down, left, or right) from the starting square;
    2. then one square diagonally, outward, from that intermediate position.
  • The horse does not jump: the adjacent orthogonal square (the horse's "leg") must be empty. If it is occupied, movement in that direction is blocked.
  • The destination square must be empty or occupied by an enemy piece, which is then captured.

3.6 Cannon

Non-capturing movement
  • The cannon moves like a chariot: any number of squares horizontally or vertically.
  • All intermediate squares must be empty.
Capture
  • The cannon must be on the same rank or column as the target enemy piece.
  • There must be exactly one piece (of either side) between the cannon and its target: this piece is called the "screen".
  • No other piece may be between the screen and the target.
  • The cannon then moves directly to the target's square and captures it.

The cannon cannot capture without a screen, nor if there is more than one piece between it and the target.

3.7 Empress

The empress combines two distinct movement modes: that of the chariot and that of the knight.

  • On each move, the empress chooses one of the following two modes:
    Chariot mode
    • The empress moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically.
    • It cannot jump over other pieces.
    • It captures by moving to a square occupied by an enemy piece.
    Knight mode
    • The empress makes an "L-shaped" move: two squares in an orthogonal direction, then one square perpendicular to that.
    • It can jump over intermediate pieces.
    • The destination square must be empty or occupied by an enemy piece, which is then captured.
  • The empress can only use one mode per move; it cannot combine them.
  • To determine attacked squares (particularly for check), consider all squares reachable by either mode.

Note: "knight mode" differs from the horse's movement described in 3.5 in that it can jump over a piece.

3.8 Soldier

Soldiers of each side advance in opposite directions:

  • South soldiers advance northward (increasing ranks: from 3 toward 8).
  • North soldiers advance southward (decreasing ranks: from 6 toward 1).

3.8.1 Before the River

  • A South soldier is "before the river" while it occupies a square on ranks 1 to 4.
  • A North soldier is "before the river" while it occupies a square on ranks 5 to 8.
  • Before the river, a soldier:
    • moves one square forward only;
    • captures in the same manner (one square forward);
    • cannot retreat or move sideways.

3.8.2 After the River

  • A South soldier has "crossed the river" once it reaches rank 5 or beyond.
  • A North soldier has "crossed the river" once it reaches rank 4 or below.
  • After the river, a soldier:
    • retains its ability to move one square forward;
    • gains the ability to move one square sideways (left or right);
    • captures forward or sideways;
    • still cannot retreat.

4. Attacked Squares, Check, and Move Legality

4.1 Attacked Square

A square is said to be attacked by a side if, applying the movement rules (sections 3.1 to 3.8) and taking obstacles into account, one of its pieces could move there to capture a hypothetical enemy piece.

  • To determine whether a square is attacked, ignore whether that move would expose that side's general to check (pins do not make a square "unattacked").
  • Soldiers attack the same way they capture:
    • before the river: one square forward;
    • after the river: one square forward or sideways.

4.2 Check

  • A general is in check if the square it occupies is attacked by at least one enemy piece.
  • A move that leaves the active player's general in check is illegal.
  • Consequently, a player cannot:
    • ignore an existing check;
    • deliberately move their general to an attacked square;
    • move a piece protecting their general if doing so exposes it to attack.

4.3 Responding to Check

When a player begins their turn with their general in check, they must play a move that removes the check. Three types of responses are possible:

  • move the general to an unattacked square;
  • capture the piece giving check;
  • interpose a piece between the attacker and the general.

If none of these responses is possible, the general is in checkmate (see section 6.1).

4.4 Negative Rules

  • There is no castling.
  • There is no dropping of captured pieces.

4.5 Prohibition of Facing Generals

The two generals are said to be "facing" when:

  • they occupy the same column;
  • no piece is between them;
  • the South general is on a lower rank than the North general.

Such a configuration is forbidden. No legal move can lead to it.

5. Soldier Promotion

  • When a soldier completes a legal move on the opponent's last rank (rank 8 for South, rank 1 for North), the player may choose to promote it.
  • Promotion is optional: the player may leave the soldier as is or promote it immediately.
  • A soldier on the last rank may be promoted later, provided the player has just moved it. This allows strategies where the player defers the promotion decision until an opportune moment.
  • Upon promotion, the soldier is removed and replaced on the same square by a piece of the player's choice from among:
    • empress,
    • guard,
    • cannon,
    • chariot,
    • bear,
    • horse.
  • Promotion to general is forbidden.
  • The choice of promoted piece is free, regardless of pieces already present or captured. A player may thus have multiple pieces of the same type.

6. End of Game

6.1 Victory by Checkmate

A side is in checkmate when:

  • its general is in check;
  • and there is no legal move that removes the check.

The side in checkmate loses the game; the opponent wins.

6.2 Stalemate

Stalemate occurs when:

  • it is a player's turn to move;
  • that player has no legal move available;
  • and that player's general is not in check.

In Xiongqi, stalemate results in a loss for the player who cannot move.

6.3 Draw by Repetition

A position is considered repeated when it occurs for the third time during the game.

Two positions are identical if:

  • the same pieces of the same sides occupy the same squares;
  • and it is the same side's turn to move.

When a position is reached for the third time, the game is declared a draw.

6.4 Draw by Insufficient Material

A draw by insufficient material occurs when neither side has the means to checkmate the opponent, regardless of the sequence of moves played. This includes positions where only the two generals remain on the board.

Such a position results in a draw.

6.5 Other Cases

Apart from the cases above, a game may also end by resignation, time expiration, or mutual agreement to draw.
